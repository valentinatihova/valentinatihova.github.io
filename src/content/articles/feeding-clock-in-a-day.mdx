## The source: twenty-four hours in a spreadsheet

The raw data is embarrassingly simple. Eight rows, two columns: a timestamp and a bottle volume in milliliters.
Nothing unusual &mdash; the same shape of log that thousands of parents keep on their phones while they learn a new
human's rhythm.

The interesting question is not how to store it. The interesting question is whether a single day's log has any
visual structure at all, or whether it is just noise. A table of eight rows hides that. A day-clock shows it in
about three seconds.

## What this visualization is NOT

This is a personal data piece, not a clinical tool. It rewards a careful reading of one day; it does not tolerate
being read as a longitudinal dataset, and it was not designed to.

<Callout type="caveat" title="What this visualization does NOT claim">
  - Not a medical or nutritional recommendation. The 124 ml average is this day's average, not a target.
  - Sample size is n = 1 day. Rhythm, averages, and the longest-gap duration will vary heavily across days and
    across babies.
  - The radial layout compresses time; adjacency on the clock does not imply causality in the underlying behavior.
  - This is a family keepsake in poster form, not a production dashboard. It intentionally privileges readability
    over statistical rigor.
</Callout>

The value of the piece is not the math. The math is trivial. The value is the **visual grammar** &mdash; a
radial data-object that holds twenty-four hours of a new life and is still readable at a glance a week, a month,
or a year later. That is what this case is really about.
