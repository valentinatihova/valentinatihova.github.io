## Problem framing

The goal is to estimate **recovery of gold** at two choke points of the beneficiation line — **rougher output** and
**final output** — from sensor and feed parameters, so operations can reject uneconomic runs before committing energy
and reagents. The rubric penalizes naive RMSE: the project defines a custom **sMAPE** on each target and a **combined**
score that weights final recovery more heavily than rougher (`0.25 × rougher_sMAPE + 0.75 × final_sMAPE`).

## Data shape and leakage risk

Three tables arrive — `train`, `test`, and `full` — with column names encoded as **`stage.parameter_type.signal`** (for
example `rougher.input.feed_size`, `final.output.concentrate_au`). Train carries late-measured **output** and
**calculation** fields that do not exist in test because those measurements simply are not available at scoring time.
Any model that silently keeps test-invisible columns in `X` would be cheating; the notebook aligns columns by
intersection and rebuilds targets by **date-indexed joins** into `full`, so rougher and final recovery on test rows are
only taken where timestamps exist in the complete log.

## Feature selection before EDA plots

The first modeling gate is correlation against both recovery columns. Features with **weak linear coupling** to either
target are dropped in bulk so later plots and grids are not dominated by noise dimensions.

## Feed size and train vs test calibration

`rougher.input.feed_size` is a good canary for covariate shift. After daily aggregation, train and test histograms stay
in the same support; aggressive outliers still deserve trimming before tree models overfit tails.

## Total element load across stages

Summing Ag, Au, Pb, and solubles into **feed**, **rougher concentrate**, and **final concentrate** totals highlights
impossible rows (near-zero mass) and long tails. The notebook filters on minimum total concentration before fitting,
then drops the engineered sums so they do not leak as redundant features.

## Modeling and evaluation (summary)

- **Targets** are recovered from `full` on the shared date index so train and test share the same label definition.
- **Features** are intersected across train/test; targets are removed from `X`.
- **Metrics**: custom `smape` wrapped in `make_scorer`, then `final_smape` for the business mix.
- **Baselines**: linear regression, grid-tuned decision tree, and dummy mean — cross-validated on train, then reported
  on test. In the captured run, **linear regression achieves the lowest combined cross-validated sMAPE** (~10.1 vs
  ~10.3 for the tuned tree on the same folds); the dummy baseline sits higher (~11.2 combined), which is the sanity
  check you want before trusting any nonlinear structure.

Test-set numbers read worse than validation — expected under temporal drift and reagent variability — but the ranking
between learned models and the constant baseline still answers the “is this model doing work?” question.

## Takeaways

Industrial tabular problems punish two shortcuts: **using future-stage columns that do not exist in deployment**, and
**ignoring concentration zeros that are really missing data**. The notebook keeps the feature contract honest, uses a
metric aligned with operations, and documents where signal lives before GridSearch spends cycles. For exact tables,
hyperparameters, and every numeric printout, stay in the repository — this page is the briefing, not the lab notebook.
